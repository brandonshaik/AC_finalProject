Why Sanctuary Cities are Important for Asylum Seekers

by Brandon Shaik, Rheaa Rao, Michael Wilson

Since the 1980s, Sanctuary Cities have popped up across the United States, giving undocumented immigrants a place to live without fear of deportation, but these havens are only part of the story for those seeking asylum.

Immigrants fleeing war and civil unrest come to the U.S., but they must apply for asylum, the right to international protection, to remain in the country legally. This process can be difficult to navigate even for those familiar with the U.S legal system.

There are two different types of asylum in the United States, affirmative and defensive. The primary difference between the two is that defensive asylum occurs after the deportation process has begun, while affirmative asylum is the general application.

As the data shows nearly half of seekers in any given year are forced to apply for asylum as a defense from deportation. This happens after the person is arrested and their illegal status is revealed. They have to opportunity to apply for asylum in the immigration courts to prevent deportation. This process can take anywhere from three to five years on average to complete whether the applicant is approved or not, according to the American Immigration Council.

Under the Reagan administration, it became difficult for immigrants to be granted asylum, so cities like Los Angeles, New York and Washington D.C. became designated as "Sanctuary Cities." These designations highlight the passage of laws that prevent local law enforcement from working with federal immigration officers to uncover a personâ€™s legal status. These laws in these cities can provide the time needed for undocumented immigrants to seek affirmative asylum, which has a higher rate of approval.

If an undocumented immigrant commits a crime in a Sanctuary City and is arrested, it is illegal for them to be questioned about their citizen status and they are to be charged and released as if they were American citizens. This prevents them from deportation and gives them time to apply and be granted asylum.

The United States granted asylum to 544,740 undocumented immigrants from 103 different countries between 1990 and 2013.
