Why Sanctuary Cities are Important for Asylum Seekers

by Brandon Shaik, Rheaa Rao, Michael Wilson

Since the 1980s, Sanctuary Cities have popped up across the United States, giving undocumented immigrants a place to live without fear of deportation.

Immigrants fleeing war and civil unrest came to the U.S. and needed to apply for asylum - the right to international protection. Under the Reagan administration, it became difficult for immigrants to be granted asylum, so cities like Los Angeles, New York and Washington D.C. became designated as "Sanctuary Cities."

If an undocumented immigrant commits a crime in a Sanctuary City and is arrested, it is illegal for them to be questioned about their citizen status and they are to be charged and released as if they were American citizens. This prevents them from deportation and gives them time to apply and be granted asylum.

The United States granted asylum to 544,740 undocumented immigrants from 103 different countries between 1990 and 2013.

There are two different types of asylum in the United States, affirmative and defensive. The primary different between the two is that defensive asylum occurs after the deportation process has begun, while affirmative asylum is the general application. Sanctuary cities provide undocumented immigrants with a higher chance of seeking affirmative asylum, which has a higher rate of approval.
